Exploratory Analysis for Shared Bikes in Chicago¶

Description¶

Chicago with a population of 2.7 Million is the 3rd Largest City in the United States of America. The City is a major transportation hub in the United States it comprises Expressways, Transit systems (metro & Buses), Passenger rail & Bicycle, and scooter sharing systems across the city. The city launched the bicycle-sharing system Divvy in 2013, and it is currently being operated by Lyft As of July 2019, Divvy operated 5800 bicycles at 608 stations. The city of Chicago has recently launched Electric scooters in addition to its fleet of Bike rides. The Bike-sharing system works on short-term rentals where users can pick up and drop their bikes at designated docking stations across the city. Each ride detail gets captured in the Divvy database and is released every month. The sharing company offers annual membership to the users, based on it the users can be divided into casual riders and members. There are 3 different kinds of bikes offered, classic, electric, and docked bike. Apart from these details

Potential Analysis¶

The dataset identifies Users as casual, or annual members. It also has latitude and longitude information for the start and end location of the ride as well as the Start time and end time. Using this dataset We can, Understand how casual riders and annual membership riders differ in bike usage. What are the most commonly used routes? Identify peaks, and lows in shared bike demands for time, date, and month of the Year.

Data Quality:¶

Divvy started releasing this data in April 2020, and has continued to release it every month. Currently, it holds 22 datasets each month. Each dataset is a CSV separate file capturing details of every ride made by the user. For this project purpose, we will be looking at data from December 2021 to November 2022. As the pool enters year's data, this gives a large sample size reflecting the population of the city.

Data Integrity:¶

The data is original as it is released by the parent company every month. Data is Current, and the source keeps on getting refreshed every month. The data I used is Divvy's Historical Trip Data to analyze and identify trends.

Ethics issues:¶

The data privacy policy prohibits sharing the rider's personally identifiable information. Each rider in this dataset is identified by their ride ID. Unfortunately, this means we do not have access to the demography of the users like age, gender, and home locations, nor can we identify purchase patterns associated with annual membership purchases.

def remove_outlier(df, col_name):
    q1 = df[col_name].quantile(0.25)
    q3 = df[col_name].quantile(0.75)
    iqr = q3-q1 #Interquartile range
    outlier = []
    for i in df[col_name]:
        if i<(q1 - 1.5 * iqr) or i>(q3 + 1.5 * iqr):
            outlier.append(i)
    outlier = pd.DataFrame(outlier,columns=['outlier'])
    print('There is {}% Outlier removed in {} according to IQR rule'.format(round((outlier.shape[0]/df.shape[0])*100,2),col_name))
    fence_low  = q1-1.5*iqr
    fence_high = q3+1.5*iqr
    df = df[(df[col_name] > fence_low) & (df[col_name] < fence_high)]
    return df

Summary¶

  1. Casual riders and anual members use Divvy Bike Share very differently.
  2. Regular riders' typical rides last longer than twice as long as those of members.
  3. We can infer from the difference in average ride length that annual members often use bike sharing for everyday commuting while casual users utilize it primarily for leisure rides on weekends.
  4. However, a predetermined number of casual users commute using bike share.
